HIV WORLD CASES

HIV (human immunodeficiency virus) is a virus that attacks cells that help the body fight infection, making a person more vulnerable to other infections and diseases. It is spread by contact with certain bodily fluids of a person with HIV, most commonly during unprotected sex (sex without a condom or HIV medicine to prevent or treat HIV), or through sharing injection drug equipment. If left untreated, HIV can lead to the disease AIDS (acquired immunodeficiency syndrome). The boxplot analysis of HIV-related death cases across WHO regions reveals significant variations. Africa shows an exceptionally wide range, from 100 to 18,250, with most cases being relatively small. The Americas, Eastern Mediterranean, and Europe have narrower ranges, with many cases near the lower end. South-East Asia has a broad range, with some countries experiencing very high numbers, while the Western Pacific also shows variability. Overall, in all regions, the distribution is positively skewed, with median values close to the minimum range, underscoring the need for ongoing efforts to reduce HIV-related mortality, particularly in regions with larger variations in case numbers.

The bar chart highlights Africa's disproportionate number of death cases due to HIV/AIDS compared to other WHO regions. This disparity can be attributed to higher HIV prevalence, limited access to healthcare and antiretroviral treatment, social and economic factors, and stigma. In contrast, regions like Europe and the Americas have made more progress in HIV prevention and treatment. Addressing these disparities requires urgent global efforts to improve access to testing, treatment, and prevention strategies in Africa to save lives and reduce the gap in HIV/AIDS-related deaths.

Number of People Living with HIV

The stacked area chart reveals a consistent increase in HIV-related living cases in Africa, reflecting progress in combating the epidemic, while other regions show less significant changes due to their lower initial numbers. The Americas display fluctuations, South-East Asia, Europe, the Western Pacific, and the Eastern Mediterranean exhibit concerning upward trends, demanding attention. Notably, the Eastern Mediterranean, despite its small chart area, experiences an extremely significant growth in active cases, where the growth is close to exponential growth or around 5 times larger growth from 2000 to 2018, emphasizing the urgency of addressing the HIV epidemic in this region. These trends highlight the ongoing global challenge of HIV and the need for sustained efforts in prevention, testing, and treatment, particularly in regions witnessing substantial increases in the number of people living with HIV.

The boxplot analysis of HIV prevalence across WHO regions reveals notable variations. Africa shows a wide range, with most cases relatively low. The Eastern Mediterranean and Europe have narrower ranges, indicating more consistent prevalence. South-East Asia has a broader range, reflecting varying prevalence, and the Western Pacific shows variability as well. The Americas, surprisingly, report an exceptionally high number of cases, exceeding Africa's maximum by tenfold, signaling a concentration of cases in specific countries and a heavy-tailed distribution. Across regions, the positively skewed distribution, with median values close to the minimum range, emphasizes the ongoing need to reduce HIV-related active cases, especially in regions with significant disparities in case numbers and prevalence rates.

Africa leads with its substantial population and higher prevalence rates, while the Americas surprisingly report more cases than South-East Asia, indicating concentrated cases in certain American countries. South-East Asia follows, showcasing varied prevalence rates across its diverse nations. Europe exhibits a more consistent pattern, the Western Pacific displays variability, and the Eastern Mediterranean demonstrates a uniform prevalence distribution. Notably, South-East Asia previously reported more HIV-related deaths than the Americas, highlighting regional complexities. The discrepancy between the Americas' higher HIV cases and South-East Asia's greater deaths can be attributed to varied testing and diagnosis approaches, differential access to antiretroviral treatment (ART), stigma-related testing delays in South-East Asia, as well as differences in HIV strains and healthcare infrastructure.

HIV trends among adults aged 15 to 49 in WHO regions vary. Europe sees an upward trend, possibly due to increased testing and risky behaviors. Africa and the Americas show downward trends thanks to successful prevention and treatment programs. The Eastern Mediterranean follows with declining cases, likely due to improved access to testing and treatment. South-East Asia and the Western Pacific fluctuate, influenced by healthcare access and changing risk behaviors. Tailored, region-specific strategies remain crucial for effective HIV management.

Overall, the positively skewed distribution suggests many countries with lower adult cases rates, but some have higher rates. Europe, despite its lower percentage, accommodates a considerable number of people living with HIV aged 15-49, thanks to effective public health responses, widespread access to antiretroviral treatment, and robust prevention measures.

ART (Antiretrovirals) Coverage

Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) is an infection that attacks the body's immune system. Acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) is the most advanced stage of the disease. HIV targets the body's white blood cells, weakening the immune system. This makes it easier to get sick with diseases like tuberculosis, infections and some cancers. HIV is spread from the body fluids of an infected person, including blood, breast milk, semen and vaginal fluids. It is not spread by kisses, hugs or sharing food. It can also spread from a mother to her baby. HIV can be treated and prevented with antiretroviral therapy (ART). Untreated HIV can progress to AIDS, often after many years. WHO now defines Advanced HIV Disease (AHD) as CD4 cell count less than 200cells/mm3 or WHO stage 3 or 4 in adults and adolescents. All children with HIV younger than 5 years of age are considered to have advanced HIV disease.
